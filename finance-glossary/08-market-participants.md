# 8. Market Participants (Who's Who)

The market is full of different players. Here's who they are, in plain terms — including the famous
**hedge fund**.

---

### Retail Investor
An everyday individual investing their own money (you, me, your neighbor).
🧠 *Analogy:* A home cook buying groceries for the family.

### Institutional Investor
A big organization that invests huge sums: pension funds, insurance companies, mutual funds, banks.
🧠 *Analogy:* A giant restaurant chain buying ingredients by the truckload — different scale, better
prices, more influence.

### Broker / Brokerage
The middleman that places your buy/sell orders on the exchange (you can't walk onto an exchange
yourself). Examples: Robinhood, Fidelity, Zerodha, Interactive Brokers.
🧠 *Analogy:* A travel agent who books the flight for you — you can't go straight to the airline's
control room.

### Dealer / Market Maker
A firm that's always ready to **both buy and sell** an instrument, providing liquidity. It earns the
**spread** (the small bid/ask gap).
🧠 *Analogy:* A currency-exchange booth at the airport — always quoting a buy price and a sell price,
making a little on each swap. Keeps the market flowing so you can always trade.

### Mutual Fund
A professionally managed pool of many people's money, usually with rules (e.g., "only US stocks").
Generally aims to grow steadily; heavily regulated; open to the public.

### Hedge Fund ⭐
A **private** investment fund for wealthy individuals and institutions that uses more aggressive,
flexible strategies to chase higher returns — including things mutual funds usually can't do, like
**short selling**, heavy **leverage**, and **derivatives**.
🧠 *Analogy:* A mutual fund is a family sedan (safe, rule-bound, for everyone). A hedge fund is a
race car (powerful, fewer rules, for experienced/wealthy passengers, higher risk).
❓ *Key points in plain terms:*
- "**Hedge**" originally meant *reducing risk* (protecting bets), but modern hedge funds often take
  *big* risks for *big* potential rewards.
- They're **lightly regulated** and usually open only to **accredited (wealthy) investors**, not the
  general public.
- Classic fee model: "**2 and 20**" — 2% of your money per year + 20% of any profits.
📌 *Example:* A hedge fund might be **long** Apple and **short** Samsung at the same time, betting
Apple outperforms — profiting whether the overall market goes up or down.

### Private Equity (PE)
Funds that buy **entire companies** (often not on the stock market), improve them, and sell later.
🧠 *Analogy:* House-flippers, but for whole businesses.

### Investment Bank
A bank that helps companies raise money (run IPOs, issue bonds), advises on mergers, and trades.
Examples: Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley.

### Custodian
A trusted institution that **safely holds** investors' assets and records who owns what.
🧠 *Analogy:* A bank vault for your shares — it doesn't decide trades, it just safekeeps and keeps
the ownership records straight.

### Prime Broker
A special broker (usually part of a big bank) that serves hedge funds — lending them money and
shares (for leverage and shorting), and handling their trades.
🧠 *Analogy:* A concierge-plus-lender for big funds.

### Clearing House
A neutral middle organization that stands between buyer and seller to guarantee the trade completes,
even if one side fails.
🧠 *Analogy:* An escrow service for trades — holds things securely so neither side gets cheated.

### Regulator
Government bodies that police the markets to keep them fair (e.g., **SEC** in the US, **SEBI** in
India, **FCA** in the UK).
🧠 *Analogy:* The referees and rule-makers of the financial game.
