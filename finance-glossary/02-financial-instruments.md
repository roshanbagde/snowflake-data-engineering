# 2. Financial Instruments

You'll hear the word **"instrument"** constantly. It just means **any tradable financial thing** —
something you can buy, hold, and sell. A stock is an instrument. So is a bond. So is an option.

🧠 *Analogy:* In a kitchen, "utensil" covers spoons, knives, and whisks. "Instrument" covers
stocks, bonds, options, and more — different tools, same umbrella word.

---

## The main types of instruments

### Equity / Stock
Ownership in a company (covered in file 01). The most familiar instrument.
❓ *Risk/reward:* Can grow a lot, but can also fall to zero if the company fails.

### Bond / Fixed Income
A **loan** you give to a company or government. They pay you interest regularly, then return your
original money on a set date.
🧠 *Analogy:* You're the bank. You lend $1,000, get $50/year interest, and your $1,000 back in 10 years.
📌 *Example:* A US Treasury bond is lending money to the US government.
❓ *Why it matters:* Generally safer and steadier than stocks, but lower growth. Called "fixed
income" because the interest payments are usually fixed.

### Cash / Money Market
Plain cash, or very short-term, very safe places to park cash that earn a tiny bit of interest.
🧠 *Analogy:* Your wallet and savings account.

### Fund (Mutual Fund)
A big shared pot where many people's money is pooled and a manager invests it across many stocks/bonds.
🧠 *Analogy:* A potluck — everyone contributes, and a chef (the manager) cooks one big meal for all.
❓ *Why it matters:* Lets a beginner own a slice of hundreds of companies at once = instant diversification.

### ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)
Like a mutual fund (a basket of many investments), but it **trades on an exchange like a single stock**,
so you can buy/sell it any time during the day.
🧠 *Analogy:* A pre-made fruit basket you can grab off the shelf with one barcode, instead of buying
each fruit separately.
📌 *Example:* An "S&P 500 ETF" lets you buy all 500 companies in one click.

### Commodity
Physical raw goods that are traded: gold, oil, wheat, coffee, natural gas.
🧠 *Analogy:* Buying and selling actual stuff from the ground or farm (usually via contracts, not
literally a truck of oil in your driveway).

### Derivative
An instrument whose value is **derived from** (based on) something else — like a stock, a commodity,
or a currency. **Options** and **futures** are derivatives.
🧠 *Analogy:* A bet on the score of a football game. The bet ticket itself has value, but that value
comes from the game, not from the ticket. (Full detail in file 06.)

### Currency / FX
Money of different countries (dollars, euros, rupees) traded against each other. (Full detail in file 07.)

---

## Quick comparison

| Instrument | What it is | Risk | Income |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stock/Equity | Ownership of a company | Higher | Maybe dividends |
| Bond | A loan to a company/government | Lower | Regular interest |
| ETF / Mutual Fund | A basket of many instruments | Medium (spread out) | Mixed |
| Commodity | Physical goods (gold, oil…) | Varies | None |
| Derivative (option/future) | A contract based on another asset | Can be very high | None directly |
| Cash | Money itself | Lowest | Tiny |

---

### Asset Class
A *category* of instruments that behave similarly. The big ones: **equities** (stocks),
**fixed income** (bonds), **commodities**, **cash**, **currencies**, and **real estate**.
🧠 *Analogy:* Food groups (fruits, grains, proteins). A balanced "diet" (portfolio) has a mix.

### Security
A fancy umbrella word for a tradable financial instrument (especially stocks and bonds). When
someone says "securities," they basically mean "tradable investments."
