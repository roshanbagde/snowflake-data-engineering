# 3. Prices and Quotes

How much something costs — and why that's trickier than a single number.

---

### Price
What one unit of an instrument is worth right now.
📌 *Example:* "AAPL is trading at $190" means one Apple share costs about $190.
❓ *Why it matters:* Prices move every second based on supply and demand — how many people want to
buy vs sell at that moment.

### Quote
The current price information for an instrument, usually showing the bid and ask (below).
🧠 *Analogy:* The little price tag display at a currency-exchange counter showing buy and sell rates.

### Bid and Ask (the two-sided price)
- **Bid** = the highest price a *buyer* is willing to pay right now.
- **Ask** (or **Offer**) = the lowest price a *seller* is willing to accept right now.
🧠 *Analogy:* At a market stall, you offer ₹90 (bid), the seller wants ₹95 (ask). A trade happens
when they meet.
📌 *Example:* Bid $190.00 / Ask $190.05.

### Spread
The gap between the bid and the ask.
📌 *Example:* Bid $190.00, Ask $190.05 → spread = $0.05.
❓ *Why it matters:* A **small** spread means lots of trading activity (easy to buy/sell). A **wide**
spread means few participants (harder, costlier to trade). The spread is also how middlemen
(market makers) earn money.

### Last Price
The price at which the most recent trade actually happened.

### Open, High, Low, Close (OHLC)
The four key prices for a time period (usually one day):
- **Open** — first traded price of the day.
- **High** — highest price reached.
- **Low** — lowest price reached.
- **Close** — last price when the market closed.
🧠 *Analogy:* A daily weather summary: morning temp, daily high, daily low, evening temp.
❓ *Why it matters:* These four numbers summarize a whole day and are the basis of most price charts
(the "candlesticks" you see).

### Volume
How many units (shares/contracts) were traded in a period.
📌 *Example:* "Apple traded 50 million shares today."
❓ *Why it matters:* High volume = lots of interest/activity. Big price moves on high volume are
considered more meaningful than moves on low volume.

### Market Capitalization ("Market Cap")
The total value of a company = share price × number of shares.
📌 *Example:* $190/share × 16 billion shares ≈ $3 trillion.
🧠 *Analogy:* The "size" of the company in the market's eyes. Big = "large cap," small = "small cap."

### Volatility
How much and how fast a price jumps around.
🧠 *Analogy:* A calm lake (low volatility) vs choppy ocean waves (high volatility).
❓ *Why it matters:* High volatility = bigger potential gains *and* losses = more risk. (More in file 10.)

### Liquidity
How easily you can buy or sell something without moving its price.
🧠 *Analogy:* Cash and gold are easy to sell quickly (liquid). A rare painting is hard to sell fast
without dropping the price (illiquid).
📌 *Example:* Apple stock is very liquid; a tiny unknown company's stock is not.

### Tick
The smallest amount a price can move (e.g., one cent).
