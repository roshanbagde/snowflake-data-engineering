# 10. Risk and Key Metrics

Investing is about balancing **reward against risk**. These are the words people use to measure and
manage risk.

---

### Risk
The chance that things don't go as hoped — that you lose money or returns are uncertain.
🧠 *Analogy:* The chance of rain on your picnic. You can't remove it, but you can prepare (bring an
umbrella = hedge).

### Volatility (recap + depth)
How much a price swings up and down. Often used as a stand-in for "risk."
🧠 *Analogy:* A bumpy road vs a smooth one. More bumps = more volatility = harder to predict.
📌 *Example:* A stock that swings ±5% daily is far more volatile than one moving ±0.5%.

### Diversification
Spreading money across many different investments so one bad one doesn't sink you.
🧠 *Analogy:* "Don't put all your eggs in one basket." If you drop one basket, the rest survive.
📌 *Example:* Owning 50 stocks across many industries instead of one stock.

### Hedge / Hedging
Making a second move that **protects** you if your main bet goes wrong — trading some upside for
safety. (This is the original meaning behind "hedge fund.")
🧠 *Analogy:* Buying travel insurance. You hope you don't need it, but it limits your loss if things
go bad.
📌 *Example:* You own Apple shares (long). You buy a **put option** on Apple so that if Apple
crashes, the put gains value and cushions your loss. That put is your hedge.

### Leverage
Using **borrowed money** to make a bigger investment than your own cash allows. Multiplies both
gains *and* losses.
🧠 *Analogy:* A mortgage: $50k of your own + $200k borrowed buys a $250k house. If it rises 10%, you
gain $25k on your $50k (a 50% gain!). If it falls 10%, you lose $25k — half your money. Same tool,
both directions.
📌 *Example:* "10x leverage" means a 1% market move = a 10% move in your account.

### Margin
The cash you must put up as a deposit/collateral when you borrow to trade (i.e., when using leverage).
- **Margin call:** if your position loses too much, the broker demands more cash — or sells you out.
🧠 *Analogy:* The security deposit on a rental. If you damage things (lose money), they may ask for more.

### Liquidity Risk
The risk you **can't sell** something quickly without a big price drop.
📌 *Example:* In a panic, even "safe" assets can become hard to sell at a fair price.

### Credit / Default Risk
The risk that a borrower (e.g., a bond issuer) **fails to pay you back**.
🧠 *Analogy:* Lending money to a friend who might not repay.

### Market Risk
The risk that the **whole market** falls and drags your investments down, no matter how good they are.

### VaR (Value at Risk)
An estimate of the *most* you'd likely lose over a period, with some confidence level.
📌 *Example:* "1-day 95% VaR = $1M" → on 95% of days, losses should stay under $1M (but 1 day in 20
could be worse).
🧠 *Analogy:* "I'm 95% sure my electric bill won't top $200 this month." A planning estimate, not a
guarantee.

### Beta
How much a stock moves compared to the overall market.
- Beta 1 = moves with the market. Beta 2 = moves twice as much (riskier). Beta 0.5 = half as much.
📌 *Example:* Market up 10%, a beta-2 stock tends to rise ~20% (and fall harder too).

### Alpha
The extra return a manager achieves **beyond** what the market gave — their skill, in a number.
📌 *Example:* Market returned 8%, your fund returned 11% → 3% alpha.

### Risk/Reward Ratio
How much you could gain vs how much you could lose on a trade.
🧠 *Analogy:* Risking $1 to possibly make $3 is a good ratio; risking $3 to make $1 is poor.

### Sharpe Ratio
A score for "return earned per unit of risk taken." Higher = better returns for the bumpiness endured.
