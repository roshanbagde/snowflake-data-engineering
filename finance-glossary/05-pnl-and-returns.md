# 5. P&L and Returns

**P&L = Profit and Loss.** The single most important question in trading: *am I making or losing
money?* This file makes P&L crystal clear.

---

### P&L (Profit and Loss / PnL)
The money you've made or lost on your positions.
- **Positive P&L** = profit (green). **Negative P&L** = loss (red).
📌 *Example:* Bought 100 shares at $190 ($19,000). Now worth $200 each ($20,000). P&L = **+$1,000**.
🧠 *Analogy:* You buy a bike for $100, its resale value is now $130 → your "P&L" on the bike is +$30.

### Realized vs Unrealized P&L (a key distinction!)
- **Unrealized P&L ("paper" profit/loss):** the gain/loss you have *on paper* while you still hold
  the position. It's not locked in — it can still change.
  📌 *Example:* You still own the shares, up $1,000 — but you haven't sold, so it's unrealized.
  🧠 *Analogy:* Your house "is worth" $50k more than you paid — but only on paper until you sell it.
- **Realized P&L:** the gain/loss you've **locked in by actually selling**. Real, final money.
  📌 *Example:* You sell the shares for $20,000 → the $1,000 profit is now realized (real cash).

> 🔑 You can have a big unrealized profit and still end up with a loss if the price drops before you
> sell. Nothing is "real" until it's realized.

### Mark-to-Market (MTM)
Revaluing your positions at the **current market price** to see what they're worth *right now*.
This is how unrealized P&L is calculated, usually at the end of each day.
🧠 *Analogy:* Checking Zillow every evening to see today's value of your house.
📌 *Example:* You hold 100 shares. Today's close is $200 → your position is "marked" at $20,000.
❓ *Why it matters:* Funds and banks mark-to-market daily so everyone knows the true current value of
their books, not just what they originally paid.

### Cost Basis (recap)
What you originally paid (your reference point). P&L = current value − cost basis.

### Return (and Return %)
Your profit expressed as a percentage of what you invested. Lets you compare investments fairly.
📌 *Example:* $1,000 profit on $19,000 invested = ~5.3% return.
🧠 *Analogy:* Two people each made $100 profit. One invested $1,000 (10% return), the other $10,000
(1% return). The percentage tells you who did *better*, not just who made more dollars.

### Gross vs Net
- **Gross:** before costs (fees, commissions, taxes).
- **Net:** after all those costs are subtracted. **Net is what you actually keep.**
📌 *Example:* Gross profit $1,000 − $50 fees − $100 tax = **$850 net**.

### Commission / Fees
What the broker charges you to execute a trade. Eats into your P&L.

### Drawdown
The drop from a peak to a low point — how far you fell from your best.
📌 *Example:* Your portfolio hit $100k, then dropped to $80k → 20% drawdown.
🧠 *Analogy:* Your highest weight vs current weight — how much you're "down" from the peak.

### Break-even
The price at which you neither make nor lose money (you'd just get back what you put in, including fees).

### ROI (Return on Investment)
Same idea as Return % — profit divided by amount invested, as a percentage.

---

## Putting it together — a full example
1. You **buy** 100 shares at $190 → cost basis $19,000, you're now **long**.
2. Price rises to $200. **Mark-to-market** values it at $20,000 → **unrealized P&L +$1,000**.
3. You **sell** all 100 at $200 → you **realize** the profit. Realized P&L = +$1,000 gross.
4. Broker charged $20 commission → **net realized P&L = +$980**.
5. On your $19,000 investment, that's about a **5.2% return**.
