# 7. Currencies and FX

**FX = Foreign Exchange** = trading one country's money for another's. It's the biggest, most active
market in the world.

---

### Currency
A country's money: US dollar (USD), euro (EUR), Indian rupee (INR), Japanese yen (JPY), British
pound (GBP).

### Exchange Rate
How much one currency is worth in another.
📌 *Example:* 1 USD = 83 INR means one dollar buys 83 rupees.
🧠 *Analogy:* A conversion rate, like "1 cup = 240 ml." It tells you how to swap one for the other.

### Currency Pair
FX is always quoted as a **pair**, because you're swapping one for another.
📌 *Example:* **EUR/USD = 1.08** means 1 euro = 1.08 dollars.
- The **first** currency (EUR) is the **base** — the one you're pricing.
- The **second** (USD) is the **quote** — the one it's priced *in*.
🧠 *Analogy:* "Apples per dollar" — you always need two things to state a rate.

### Appreciation / Depreciation
- **Appreciate:** a currency gets *stronger* (buys more of another).
- **Depreciate:** a currency gets *weaker* (buys less).
📌 *Example:* If USD/INR goes from 83 to 85, the dollar appreciated (stronger) and the rupee
depreciated (weaker).

### FX Risk / Currency Risk
The risk that exchange-rate changes hurt you when you deal across currencies.
📌 *Example:* A US fund buys European stocks. Even if the stocks rise, if the euro **weakens** vs the
dollar, the fund could still lose money when converting back to dollars.
🧠 *Analogy:* You're owed money in a foreign currency; if that currency drops before you get paid,
your payment is worth less at home.

### Spot vs Forward (in FX)
- **Spot rate:** the exchange rate for swapping *right now*.
- **Forward rate:** an agreed rate to swap on a *future* date (used to lock in / hedge FX risk).

### Hedging FX
Using contracts (forwards, options) to protect against bad currency moves.
📌 *Example:* The US fund buying European stocks can lock today's EUR/USD rate with a forward, so a
falling euro won't surprise them later.

### Base Currency (of a fund/company)
The "home" currency a fund reports everything in.
📌 *Example:* A US fund's base currency is USD, so all its positions are eventually converted to
dollars for reporting — and that conversion is where FX risk and **back-office** currency reporting
come in (see file 09).

### Pip
The smallest standard move in an FX rate (often the 4th decimal place).
🧠 *Analogy:* The "cents" of the FX world — a tiny unit traders watch closely.
